A field guide to markets

The Basics of Stocks

What ownership means, how markets match buyers and sellers, why prices move, and how stocks connect to options, futures, indices, and ETFs.

25 chapters · beginner to market structure

Start with the first section, then move at your own pace.

01 · Foundations

What a Stock Is

A stock is a unit of ownership in a corporation. When a company issues shares, it divides its equity into claims that can be bought and sold. A shareholder owns a slice of the company’s equity—not a particular office, product, or bank account.

Think of it this way: the company is the operating business; the stock is the tradable ownership claim on that business.

Why companies issue shares

Companies sell equity to raise capital without taking on a loan that must be repaid. That capital can fund research, factories, hiring, acquisitions, or working capital. In exchange, existing owners share future upside and control.

Stock price versus company value

Price is the amount the market is paying per share right now. Value is an estimate of what the entire business may be worth based on future cash flows, assets, growth, and risk. They can differ for long periods.

02 · Foundations

What Owning a Stock Lets You Do

Participate in upside

If the market value of the business rises, your shares may appreciate. Your return is usually measured as price change plus income received.

Receive dividends

Some companies distribute part of their profits or cash to shareholders. Dividends are optional for common stock and can be cut.

Vote on key matters

Common shareholders may vote on directors, major transactions, and other corporate proposals, depending on the share class.

Hold a residual claim

In liquidation, shareholders stand behind creditors and preferred holders. This is why common equity can offer upside but carries real downside.

Limited liability generally means an investor cannot lose more than the amount invested in ordinary shares. It does not mean the investment itself is safe.

03 · Foundations

FeatureCommon stockPreferred stock

Preferred stock is not the same as a bond: dividends may still be deferred or canceled, and the security remains equity. Read the prospectus for conversion, call, cumulative-dividend, and voting terms.

04 · Foundations

Shares Outstanding, Float, and Dilution

Shares outstanding are the shares currently held by all shareholders, including insiders and institutions. Float is the portion generally available for public trading. A company may have many outstanding shares but a smaller float.

How ownership changes

Issuing new shares raises capital but dilutes each existing shareholder’s percentage ownership. Stock-based compensation, acquisitions paid in shares, and employee option exercises can also increase the share count.

Ownership percentage = your shares ÷ total shares outstanding

Buybacks reduce shares outstanding when the company repurchases stock. The effect on value per share depends on the price paid, financing, and what the company could have done with that cash.

05 · Price formation

Why Stock Prices Move

At any moment, a stock price is the price at which the next willing buyer and seller agree. Over time, that price responds to changing expectations about the company and the environment around it.

Expected cash flows

Revenue, margins, reinvestment, free cash flow, and the odds of future growth.

Interest rates

Higher discount rates usually reduce the present value of distant cash flows, especially for long-duration growth stocks.

Risk and liquidity

Investors demand compensation for uncertainty, while thin trading can amplify price movement.

Positioning and sentiment

Flows, short interest, leverage, narratives, and forced buying or selling can move price before fundamentals catch up.

News matters because it changes expectations—not because a headline has a fixed effect on price.

06 · Issuance

Primary Market vs. Secondary Market

In the primary market, a company sells newly issued shares and receives the proceeds. IPOs, follow-on offerings, and private placements are primary-market transactions.

In the secondary market, investors trade existing shares with one another. The company normally receives no money when you buy a share on an exchange, but the secondary market gives the ownership claim liquidity and a continuously updated price.

Key distinction: the company raises capital in issuance; investors discover price and transfer risk in trading.
07 · Trade lifecycle

How a Stock Trade Actually Works

1 · You submit

Your broker receives an order with a symbol, side, quantity, order type, and time-in-force.

2 · The broker routes

The order may go to an exchange, market maker, electronic network, or another venue.

3 · The market matches

A compatible buy and sell order execute at an available price, subject to priority rules.

4 · It clears and settles

Clearing firms manage obligations; settlement transfers cash and securities. U.S. applicable securities generally settle T+1.

Execution is not the same as settlement. You can see a fill immediately while the final delivery of cash and shares completes later.

08 · Microstructure

The Order Book, Bid/Ask Spread, and Liquidity

The order book lists resting buy and sell interest. The highest buy is the bid; the lowest sell is the ask. The difference is the spread.

TermMeaningWhy it matters
DepthOrders available at nearby pricesMore depth can absorb size with less impact
SpreadAsk minus bidA direct trading cost before fees
SlippageDifference between expected and executed priceWorsens in volatility or thin markets
LiquidityAbility to trade size quickly near the displayed priceDetermines execution quality

A quote is not a promise that your entire order will fill there. Large market orders may consume several price levels.

09 · Microstructure

Exchanges, Market Makers, and Auctions

Exchanges provide rules, matching systems, listings, and transparency. Market makers continuously quote buy and sell prices, taking inventory risk in exchange for the spread and other compensation.

Opening and closing auctions concentrate orders at a single clearing price. This helps absorb overnight information and establish reference prices. Outside the central book, alternative trading systems and internalization can also execute orders.

Priority generally matters: price comes first, then time among orders at the same price. Exact rules vary by venue and order type.
10 · United States

U.S. Stock Market Structure

The U.S. is a network of venues connected by regulation, routing, clearing, settlement, and consolidated market data. NYSE and Nasdaq are prominent exchanges; Cboe and other venues also operate exchanges and trading systems.

The plumbing

Broker-dealers handle customer orders. Exchanges and alternative venues match trades. Clearing agencies net obligations and manage counterparty risk. Depositories hold securities and support settlement. The SEC oversees the securities markets; FINRA oversees broker-dealers and many trading practices.

What a quote means

Public data may come through consolidated feeds or faster proprietary feeds. Brokers have execution-quality obligations, but the displayed best quote may change before an order arrives.

Risk controls: U.S. markets use individual-stock limit-up/limit-down bands and market-wide circuit breakers that can pause trading during extreme moves.
11 · Europe

European Stock Market Structure

Europe is not one uniform exchange. It is a collection of national markets, cross-border venues, currencies, listing regimes, and investor-access rules. MiFID II shaped how many European venues classify, route, report, and execute trades.

Euronext links markets including Amsterdam, Brussels, Dublin, Lisbon, Milan, Oslo, and Paris on a shared platform. Deutsche Börse, SIX, BME, Nasdaq Nordic, the London Stock Exchange, and other venues serve their own ecosystems.

What makes it different

Liquidity can fragment across primary exchanges, multilateral trading facilities, systematic internalisers, and dark pools. A company’s listing venue, home market, reporting currency, and index membership can all affect how it trades.

The United Kingdom has its own post-Brexit regulatory framework; it should be treated as a major European market, but not assumed to be governed identically to EU markets.

12 · Asia-Pacific

Asian Stock Market Structure

Asian markets span different legal systems, currencies, settlement cycles, trading hours, and foreign-ownership rules. Tokyo, Hong Kong, Shanghai, Shenzhen, Seoul, Singapore, Mumbai, Taipei, and Sydney each have their own market design.

Access

Some markets impose account, quota, disclosure, or ownership limits on foreign investors.

Sessions

Opening auctions, lunch breaks, night sessions, and local holidays can alter liquidity and price discovery.

Volatility controls

Price limits, special quotes, and trading halts are used in several markets to slow abrupt moves.

Cross-border risk

Currency, geopolitics, capital controls, and different accounting or disclosure standards matter.

“Asian market” is therefore a regional label, not a single trading system.

13 · Time

Market Hours and Global Price Discovery

Each market has a local session, opening and closing procedures, holidays, and sometimes pre-market or after-hours trading. The U.S. core session, for example, is typically 9:30 a.m. to 4:00 p.m. Eastern Time.

When one market is closed, related instruments can keep trading elsewhere: index futures, ADRs, currencies, bonds, and commodities may react first. The next cash-market open then incorporates that information, sometimes through a gap.

Practical implication: always convert times to the local exchange calendar and check whether the instrument is trading in its most liquid session.

Time-in-force instructions include day, good-til-canceled, immediate-or-cancel, and fill-or-kill. Extended-hours trading usually has thinner liquidity and wider spreads.

15 · Cash instruments

Spot / Cash Markets

“Spot” means trading the underlying asset for near-term delivery or settlement at the current market price. Buying a stock or a physically backed ETF in a cash account is a spot-style exposure: you exchange cash for an ownership interest in the asset.

Spot does not mean risk-free or literally instantaneous. Settlement rules still apply, and the asset can move between execution and settlement. Spot markets are distinct from derivatives whose value is linked to an underlying instrument.

16 · Leverage

Short Selling and Margin

To short a stock, an investor borrows shares, sells them, and later buys shares to return to the lender. If the price falls, the short may profit; if it rises, the loss can be very large because the stock has no fixed upper limit.

Margin means borrowing against securities or posting collateral. It magnifies gains and losses and can create margin calls, forced liquidation, interest expense, and losses beyond the initial cash deposit.

Position sizing is the first risk control. A stop cannot guarantee an exit price during a gap or disorderly market.
17 · Valuation

Basic Valuation Concepts

Valuation asks: what is the market price relative to the business’s earnings, sales, assets, cash generation, and future potential?

Market capitalization

share price × shares outstanding. The equity value of the company.

Enterprise value

A capital-structure-aware value, commonly equity value plus debt and other claims, less cash.

P/E

Price relative to earnings. Useful for profitable, comparable businesses; sensitive to accounting and cycle effects.

EV/EBITDA

Enterprise value relative to operating earnings before certain charges; useful for comparing capital structures, with caveats.

Price/sales

Useful when profits are negative, but says little about future margins by itself.

Free-cash-flow yield

Free cash flow divided by market value; a way to compare cash generation with price.

18 · Valuation

From Ratios to a Valuation

Relative valuation compares a company with peers, its own history, or a sector. Absolute valuation estimates the present value of future cash flows.

Value today = Σ [future cash flow ÷ (1 + discount rate)time]

A discounted cash flow model is not a truth machine. Small changes to growth, margins, terminal value, or discount rate can produce large changes in the estimate.

Useful cross-checks

Compare multiple methods: P/E or EV/EBITDA, balance-sheet strength, free-cash-flow yield, dividend capacity, and a conservative DCF. Ask which assumption the current price already requires.

19 · Styles

Growth, Value, and Quality

These labels describe tendencies, not sealed categories.

Growth

Investors pay for expected expansion in revenue, earnings, or cash flow. The risk is that expectations outrun delivery.

Value

Shares appear inexpensive relative to earnings, assets, or cash flow. The risk is a value trap: the business may deserve a low multiple.

Quality

Durable margins, strong returns on capital, sound balance sheets, and reliable management.

Income

Dividend or distribution yield is emphasized, but high yield can signal financial stress or a falling price.

Good analysis asks why the valuation exists and what would make the market revise it.

20 · Methods

Technical Analysis vs. Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis studies the business: financial statements, industry structure, competitive advantage, management, valuation, and catalysts.

Technical analysis studies market behavior: price, volume, trends, volatility, support and resistance, and patterns. It can help with timing and risk definition, but a chart does not remove business risk.

They answer different questions: fundamentals ask “what might this be worth?” Technicals ask “how is the market behaving around it?”
21 · Derivatives

Options

An option gives its buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a strike price before or at expiration. The buyer pays a premium; the seller receives it and takes on an obligation.

ContractRight of buyerTypical view
CallBuy the underlying at the strikeUpside exposure
PutSell the underlying at the strikeDownside protection or bearish exposure

Intrinsic value is what an option would be worth if it expired now. Time value reflects remaining time and uncertainty. Delta, gamma, theta, and vega describe how price may respond to the underlying, time, and implied volatility.

Options can hedge, express a defined view, or generate income. Leverage cuts both ways; short options can carry assignment, gap, and theoretically large-loss risk.

22 · Derivatives

Futures

A futures contract is a standardized, legally binding agreement to buy or sell an underlying asset or reference value at a specified future date or delivery month. Contracts trade on organized exchanges and are cleared through a central counterparty.

Notional value

The economic size controlled by the contract, often far larger than the cash margin posted.

Margin

Performance collateral, not a down payment. Positions are marked to market and gains or losses settle daily.

Expiration and roll

Many traders close or roll before expiry, which can create a new price and transaction cost.

Contango / backwardation

Futures can trade above or below spot, affecting returns for a rolling strategy.

Futures are used for hedging, speculation, and efficient exposure to equity indices, rates, currencies, energy, metals, and agriculture.

23 · Benchmarks

Indices

An index is a rules-based basket or calculation designed to represent a market, sector, factor, or theme. You cannot normally buy an index directly; products track it.

Market-cap weighted

Larger companies have larger weights. Examples include the S&P 500, Nasdaq Composite, STOXX Europe 600, TOPIX, and CSI 300.

Price weighted

Higher-priced shares have more influence regardless of company size. The Dow Jones Industrial Average is the familiar example.

Equal weighted

Each constituent begins with a similar weight; the portfolio must rebalance to maintain it.

Index methodology

Rules decide inclusion, free float, rebalancing, corporate actions, and treatment of dividends.

Benchmark return can differ from a headline price chart depending on whether dividends, withholding taxes, and fees are included.

24 · Funds

ETFs

An exchange-traded fund holds a portfolio and trades throughout the day like a stock. Many ETFs track an index, but some are actively managed, leveraged, inverse, or focused on a narrow theme.

NAV

Net asset value is the per-share value of the fund’s holdings after liabilities.

Creation and redemption

Authorized participants exchange baskets of securities for ETF shares, helping align market price with NAV.

Costs

Expense ratio, bid/ask spread, commissions, taxes, and tracking difference all matter.

Risks

Concentration, leverage decay, inverse exposure, liquidity mismatch, and premium/discount to NAV.

An ETF can be diversified, but “ETF” describes a wrapper—not the risk level of what is inside it.

26 · Process

Practical Stock Analysis Checklist

Business

What does it sell? Who pays? Is the market growing? What protects the economics?

Financials

Inspect revenue quality, margins, cash flow, debt, dilution, returns on capital, and capital allocation.

Valuation

What does the current price assume about growth, margins, rates, and terminal value?

Catalyst

What could change expectations: earnings, product launches, regulation, an industry cycle, or a strategic action?

Market structure

Check liquidity, spread, float, short interest, earnings dates, and whether your order can be executed cleanly.

Risk plan

Define position size, time horizon, invalidation, maximum loss, and what would make you exit or reassess.

27 · Closing thought

The Market Is a Machine for Price Discovery

Stocks turn business ownership into a liquid, continuously repriced claim. Exchanges and brokers provide the rails. Order books determine immediate execution. Valuation frames the longer-term question. Derivatives and funds reshape the way exposure can be packaged.

The useful habit is to separate the layers: what you own, what it is worth, how it trades, what can move it, and how much risk you are willing to carry.

Before any trade: know the instrument, the order, the exposure, the liquidity, and the condition that would prove your thesis wrong.
